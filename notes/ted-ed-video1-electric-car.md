## Electric Cars: Why They Disappeared and Why They Are Coming Back

### Early Success of Electric Cars

* Electric cars are not a new invention. One of the first commercial electric cars was the **Columbia Motor Carriage**, introduced by **Albert Pope** in the late 1800s.
* Around 1900, electric cars were very popular in America and even outsold gasoline-powered cars.
* They were clean, quiet, and easy to operate compared to early gasoline cars.

### Why Electric Cars Disappeared

The biggest problem was **battery technology**.

* Early electric cars used **lead-acid batteries**:

  * Cheap to make.
  * Very heavy.
  * Stored little energy.
  * Limited driving range (usually less than 70 km).

* Even **Henry Ford** and **Thomas Edison** could not develop an affordable electric car with better batteries.

Meanwhile, gasoline cars improved:

* Became more reliable.
* Fuel was widely available in hardware stores and pharmacies.
* Allowed longer journeys, which appealed to wealthy adventurers.

### Marketing Failure

* Electric cars were advertised mainly to women because they:

  * Were clean and quiet.
  * Did not require physical strength to start.
* Since most drivers at the time were men, this limited their market.
* Electric cars became a niche product and eventually disappeared.

---

### Attempts to Revive Electric Cars

#### 1970s Oil Crisis

* Rising oil prices increased interest in alternatives.
* However, battery technology had not improved enough, so electric cars remained impractical.

#### 1990s: General Motors EV1

* **General Motors** launched the EV1.
* The car gained a loyal following.
* However, the company eventually discontinued it.
* Critics argued that promoting EVs highlighted how polluting the company's other vehicles were.

### Role of the Car and Oil Industries

* Internal combustion engine vehicles generated huge profits for carmakers and oil companies.
* Many believe these industries had little incentive to support electric vehicles.

---

## Why Electric Cars Are Returning

### 1. Better Batteries

Modern EVs use **lithium-ion batteries**:

* Store more energy.
* Are lighter.
* Allow much longer driving ranges.
* Make electric cars practical for everyday use.

### 2. Climate Change Concerns

* Society is now much more aware of climate change.
* Burning fossil fuels releases carbon dioxide (CO₂), which contributes to global warming.
* Cars produce about **10% of energy-related CO₂ emissions** worldwide.
* Electric vehicles help reduce these emissions.

### Environmental Benefits

* Over their lifetime, EVs can produce **up to 70% fewer emissions** than gasoline cars, depending on how electricity is generated.
* Governments are encouraging cleaner transport through:

  * EV subsidies.
  * Tax benefits.
  * Planned bans on new gasoline and diesel cars.

---

## Growth of the EV Market

### Car Manufacturers

Many major car companies are electrifying their vehicle fleets and investing heavily in EV technology.

### Consumer Demand

* EV sales are rising rapidly, especially in:

  * Europe
  * China
* More than half of car owners say their next vehicle could be electric.

### Current Situation

* About **1.3 billion passenger vehicles** exist worldwide.
* Only about **1.5%** are electric, showing that the transition is still in its early stages.

---

## The S-Curve of Technology Adoption

Most new technologies follow an **S-curve**:

1. **Emergence** – Slow initial growth.
2. **Diffusion** – Rapid adoption.
3. **Culmination** – Growth slows as the market becomes saturated.

Examples:

* Telephones
* TVs
* Computers
* Smartphones

### EVs and the S-Curve

* In 2010, less than 1% of new cars sold were electric.
* By 2021, nearly 9% of new car sales were electric.
* Experts believe EVs are moving from the emergence stage into rapid diffusion.
* Some forecasts suggest EVs could make up around **50% of new car sales by 2030**.

---

## Challenges Ahead

### Charging Infrastructure

* More charging stations are needed.
* Charging systems should be standardized.
* Drivers need reliable access to charging everywhere.

### Electricity Grid

* Power grids must handle increased electricity demand from millions of EVs.

### Uneven Global Adoption

* Europe is approaching one-quarter of new car sales being electric.
* China adds millions of EVs every year.
* India has far fewer EVs and less than 1% of new vehicle sales are electric.

### Cost of EVs

* Electric cars are still expensive.
* Batteries are the most costly component.
* Prices are falling rapidly and are expected to become similar to gasoline cars in the future.

### Government Support

* Subsidies can speed up EV adoption.
* **Norway** is a leading example:

  * Strong incentives for many years.
  * More than 70% of new car sales are electric.

### Raw Materials

EV batteries require materials such as:

* Lithium
* Cobalt

Important questions:

* How can mining be made more sustainable?
* Can alternative battery materials be developed?

---

## Key Takeaways

* Electric cars were popular before gasoline cars but disappeared mainly because of poor battery technology.
* Modern lithium-ion batteries and climate concerns have driven their comeback.
* EV sales are growing rapidly and may have reached a global tipping point.
* Major challenges remain: charging infrastructure, electricity supply, costs, and raw material sourcing.
* The widespread shift from gasoline cars to electric vehicles now appears increasingly inevitable.
