## Structures of Power and Decision-Making (SDG 1.5)

**Who's in charge of achieving the SDGs?** The honest answer: **nobody — and everybody.** There is no single government, no UN, no G20 that calls the shots. This is a **multi-stakeholder process** across 193 countries, thousands of cities, millions of businesses, and 7.6 billion people, with no one conductor.

### The Conductor-less Orchestra

* Think of a **conductor-less orchestra** — no one person waves the baton, yet beautiful music is made.
* How? Two things:
  * **Same sheet music** — every musician plays from the same score. For the SDGs, that's the **17 Goals + the Paris Climate Agreement**.
  * **They watch each other** — constantly checking tempo, cuing each other, goading each other forward.
* That's the model for global sustainable development: **shared goals + mutual accountability**.

### The Four Stakeholders

**1. Government** (most complex)
* Divided **horizontally**: ministries, courts, legislature, executive.
* Divided **vertically**: local → provincial → national → regional (EU, African Union, ASEAN) → global (UN General Assembly, treaties).
* Roles: public investment in infrastructure (roads, power, broadband), provision of public services (schools, clinics), regulation (no pollution, no fraud, no trafficking), long-term planning, organising public deliberation, public-private partnerships.

**2. Business**
* Core role: produce goods and services — but responsible to **all stakeholders** (shareholders, workers, suppliers, customers, community, supply chain), not just shareholders.
* Shareholder-only thinking is **not consistent with sustainable development**.
* Key role in R&D and breakthrough technologies (e.g. wind power companies) — but shift from fossil fuels to clean energy requires **co-investment** with government.

**3. Civil Society** (NGOs, universities, think tanks, charities, religious orgs)
* **Consumer activism** — buy sustainably, say no to companies not following SDG principles.
* **Citizen accountability** — hold governments accountable for SDG progress and reporting.
* **Universities** — teach, research, run demonstration projects, convene experts for differential diagnoses.
* **Investor activism** — divestment movement: pressure pension funds, church funds, university endowments to stop investing in polluting companies ("bad investment + contrary to SDGs").

**4. The United Nations**
* Not the conductor — but the **most legitimate global institution** (post-WWII, includes almost all countries).
* Hosts negotiations, sets standards, houses specialised agencies: **WMO** (weather/climate), **WHO** (health), **WTO** (trade).

### Subsidiarity — Which Level Decides?

> Solve problems **as close to the people as possible**.

* **Local level** → schools, neighbourhood services.
* **National level** → railway networks, national infrastructure.
* **Trans-national** → shared rivers (dams, pollution, flooding) need a multi-country body.
* **Global** → climate change needs a global treaty (Paris Agreement); extreme poverty needs global commitment because poor countries can't escape alone.

### Key Message

No one is in charge, but **we all have the same sheet music**. The SDGs only work if every sector — government, business, civil society, and the UN — understands its comparative role and watches all the others to make sure they're doing their part.
