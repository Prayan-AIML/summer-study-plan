## Origins of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

The SDGs were created to give the world a **clear set of goals** that can guide governments, businesses, and citizens toward a common future. The idea is that when people know exactly what they are working toward, progress becomes easier. As **John F. Kennedy** said, clear goals help people see hope and move toward a shared objective. The SDGs aim to guide the world toward **shared prosperity, social inclusion, and environmental sustainability**.

### Early Beginnings (1972)

The origins of the SDGs can be traced back to **1972**, when the United Nations held the **United Nations Conference on the Human Environment** in Stockholm. This was the first major international meeting to recognize that economic growth and environmental protection were becoming increasingly connected. Countries realized that human activities were putting growing pressure on the planet.

The same year, a famous book called **The Limits to Growth** was published. Using computer models, it warned that continued economic growth with existing technologies could eventually overwhelm Earth's natural systems.

### The Idea of Sustainable Development

Fifteen years later, the **Brundtland Commission** introduced the concept of **sustainable development**. It defined sustainable development as:

> Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

This introduced the idea of **intergenerational responsibility**—the current generation must use resources wisely so future generations can also meet their needs.

### The Earth Summit (1992)

In 1992, world leaders met at the **Earth Summit** in Rio de Janeiro. The summit officially adopted sustainable development as a global goal and produced three major environmental agreements:

* **United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change** (Climate Change)
* **Convention on Biological Diversity** (Biodiversity Protection)
* **United Nations Convention to Combat Desertification** (Combating Desertification)

These agreements aimed to protect the environment on a global scale.

### Rio+20 and the Birth of the SDGs

By 2012, at the **Rio+20 Conference**, governments realized that progress had been slower than expected. Many environmental problems were worsening, and the earlier agreements were not being fully implemented.

At the same time, countries had experience with the **Millennium Development Goals** (MDGs), which focused mainly on reducing poverty from 2000–2015. Governments decided to build on this idea by creating a broader set of goals that would include economic, social, and environmental issues. This led to the proposal for the Sustainable Development Goals.

### Creating the 17 Goals

After Rio+20, countries spent three years negotiating the new goals. Initially, there were around **300 proposed goals**, but these were gradually combined and simplified.

By 2015, countries agreed on:

* **17 Sustainable Development Goals**
* **169 Targets**

These goals became part of **Agenda 2030**, the global plan for sustainable development from 2015–2030.

### Agenda 2030 and the Paris Climate Agreement

On **25 September 2015**, all **193 UN member states** adopted the SDGs and Agenda 2030. A few months later, on **12 December 2015**, countries adopted the **Paris Climate Agreement**.

The Paris Agreement is closely linked to **SDG 13 (Climate Action)** and aims to tackle human-induced climate change. Together, **Agenda 2030**, the **17 SDGs**, and the **Paris Climate Agreement** form the main framework for global cooperation on sustainable development.

### Key Dates to Remember

* **1972** – Stockholm Conference & *Limits to Growth*
* **1987** – Brundtland Commission defines sustainable development
* **1992** – Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro
* **2000–2015** – Millennium Development Goals (MDGs)
* **2012** – Rio+20 Conference proposes SDGs
* **25 Sept 2015** – SDGs and Agenda 2030 adopted
* **12 Dec 2015** – Paris Climate Agreement adopted
